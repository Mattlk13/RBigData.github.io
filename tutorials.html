# Introductory Resources ## pbdDEMO One of the best resources we have for pbdR is the [pbdDEMO package](http://cran.r-project.org/package=pbdDEMO) and its [vignette](https://github.com/wrathematics/pbdDEMO/blob/master/inst/doc/pbdDEMO-guide.pdf?raw=true). Whether you are an experienced parallel programmer or a complete novice, this will get you up to speed on MPI, running in batch, the SPMD paradigm, and utilizing the pbdR tools. ## Tutorials Slides from our most recent full tutorial: - [Slides](http://rbigdata.github.io/tutorials/user2014/pbdr.pdf?raw=true) However, for self-study we recommend using the [pbdDEMO package](http://cran.r-project.org/package=pbdDEMO) and its [vignette](https://github.com/wrathematics/pbdDEMO/blob/master/inst/doc/pbdDEMO-guide.pdf?raw=true). ## HPSC pbdR Tutorials Our sister site, [High Performance Statistical Computing (HPSC)](http://thirteen-01.stat.iastate.edu/snoweye/hpsc/), has some excellent, real world tutorials using the well-known Iris data set. This is a small data set, and is not meant to be a demonstration of pbdR's scaling prowess. Instead, this is a way to see how you might take a serial code and move it over to using our pbdR tools. The current tutorials available are: - [Clustering](http://thirteen-01.stat.iastate.edu/snoweye/pbdr/?item=tutorial) - [Bootstrapping](http://thirteen-01.stat.iastate.edu/snoweye/pbdr/?item=tutorial2) Currently the Iris tutorial above is used in the "Introducing R" tutorial slides. ## Introductory R Most of the materials on this website are either programming language agnostic, or assume you already know and love R. If you are a complete beginner to using R, there are many great resources available to you. In no particular order, here are a few you may find useful: - [Introduction to Probability and Statistics Using R](http://cran.r-project.org/web/packages/IPSUR/vignettes/IPSUR.pdf) by G. Jay Kerns. - [An Introduction to R](http://cran.r-project.org/doc/manuals/R-intro.pdf) by the R Core Team. - [The R Inferno](http://www.burns-stat.com/pages/Tutor/R_inferno.pdf) by Patrick Burns. - Norm Matloff's [R for Programmers](http://heather.cs.ucdavis.edu/~matloff/R/RProg.pdf) (which are an early draft of his excellent book [The Art of R Programming](http://nostarch.com/artofr.htm). ## Introduction to Parallel Computing If you want a very thorough, general introduction to parallel computing, then the article [Introduction to Parallel Computing](https://computing.llnl.gov/tutorials/parallel_comp/#ModelsData) by Blaise Barney of [LLNL](https://www.llnl.gov/) is a great place to start. Neither the author nor the content of that article are affiliated with team pbdR, but it is a great place to start.
